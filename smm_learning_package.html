Origins & Intellectual History

Brenda Dervin began developing Sense-Making Methodology in the early 1970s during her doctoral and early faculty work, grounded in dissatisfaction with how communication and information science treated information as a transmissible commodity. Her intellectual formation drew from several traditions simultaneously:

InfluenceWhat Dervin BorrowedWhat She Rejected or Transformed
Shannon-Weaver transmission modelCommunication as processInformation as a fixed, objective "thing" that transfers intact
Carter's information-seeking researchUsers have genuine information needsNeeds as static, predictable, topic-based
Phenomenology (Schutz, Husserl)Experience is situated, time-bound, subjectiveObserver-independent "reality"
Constructivism (Kelly, Piaget)Knowledge is constructed, not receivedLearning as passive absorption
Critical communication theoryPower shapes information access and meaningNeutral, value-free information science
Feminist epistemology (later)Subjectivity and standpoint matterUniversal, disembodied "objectivity"
Historical anchor

Dervin's early empirical work (1970s–80s) was often funded by communication research on public information campaigns — work that revealed repeatedly how target audiences failed to use "information" designed for them. SMM emerged partly as an explanatory framework: people weren't failing to receive information; the transmission model itself was wrong.

Philosophical Assumptions

SMM is not merely a research method — it rests on a distinctive ontological and epistemological foundation. These assumptions are not optional background; they are constitutive of the methodology.

1

Reality is discontinuous. The world does not present itself as a smooth, gapless surface. It is riddled with gaps, contradictions, ambiguities, and absences. Discontinuity is not an exception to be explained away — it is the fundamental condition of human experience.

2

Information is constructed, not found. There are no objective "packets" of information floating in the environment waiting to be retrieved. What counts as information is constructed in the act of making sense — it is always information-for-a-gap-in-a-situation.

3

Time and space are constitutive, not contextual. The specific moment and place in which a person finds themselves is not merely backdrop. Situation fundamentally shapes what counts as sense, what counts as a gap, and what bridges are available.

4

People are active sense-makers, not passive receivers. The human being is positioned as the agent who makes sense — using internal resources (memory, emotion, imagination) and external resources (information, people, institutions).

5

Universality of procedure, uniqueness of instance. Every human being engages in sense-making (universal procedure), but the specific sense made is always unique to the situation, the person, and the moment (unique instance). This prevents both over-generalization and radical relativism.

6

Subjectivity is not a confound — it is the datum. The internal experience of the person (confusion, emotion, intuition) is not noise to be controlled for; it is what the methodology is designed to capture and understand.

7

Both internal and external orders are real. Neither pure subjectivism (only inner experience is real) nor pure objectivism (only external facts are real) is adequate. SMM operates in the space between.

Key Concepts

Concept 01

Situation

The specific time-space context in which a person finds themselves. Situations create, constrain, and define the sense-making that is possible. Not mere background — the situation is the operative ground of all sense-making activity.

Concept 02

Gap

The discontinuity that interrupts forward movement. A question, confusion, barrier, uncertainty, dilemma, or absence. Gaps are the engine of sense-making — without a gap, no sense-making is needed. Gaps can be cognitive, emotional, or situational.

Concept 03

Bridge

The sense constructed to cross the gap. Bridges may be ideas, information found, memories recalled, emotional resolutions, social connections, or procedural steps. Bridges are built, not received. They may succeed, fail, or generate new gaps.

Concept 04

Outcome / Use

What results from bridging. Outcomes include: help (movement forward), hurt (movement blocked), confusion (new gap created), and use (what the person does with the bridge — decides, acts, connects, evaluates, feels). Multiple uses can follow one bridge.

Concept 05

Movement Through Time-Space

The root metaphor: a person moving through time and space encounters a gap and must bridge it to continue. Movement is not abstract — it is embodied, sequential, and situated. Sense-making is always in service of continued movement through a specific life journey.

The Gap: Taxonomy of Types

One of SMM's most practically useful contributions is the rich taxonomy of gap types Dervin developed. Rather than asking users "what information do you need?" (which presupposes topic-based needs), SMM asks about the nature of the gap itself.

Gap TypeDescriptionExample
DecisionFaced with choice, unclear how to decide"I need to choose between two career paths"
BarrierExternal obstacle blocking movement"I can't get access to the system I need"
ProblematicSomething is wrong but unclear what"Something feels off but I can't articulate it"
RoleUnclear what to do in a given role/situation"I don't know how to behave in this meeting"
Spin-outConfusion or disorientation, no clear question"I'm completely overwhelmed and don't know where to start"
WashoutInformation received but doesn't help"I got a lot of information but none of it addresses what I need"

Sense-Making vs. Sense-Unmaking

In her later work (primarily from the 1990s onward), Dervin complicated the model by adding sense-unmaking — the recognition that people do not only construct sense, but must also deconstruct it.

Sense-Unmaking Defined

Sense-unmaking refers to the deliberate or forced dismantling of existing sense structures. People must forget, unlearn, challenge assumptions, and destabilize established understandings in order to move through new situations. It is not the failure of sense-making — it is a constitutive part of the process.

Sense-unmaking is particularly relevant for:

  • Critical and creative thinking, which requires challenging existing frames
  • Transformative learning (Mezirow), where old sense is dismantled before new sense is built
  • Institutional power analysis: dominant groups impose sense structures that marginalized groups must actively unmake to survive
  • Organizational change, where old procedures and assumptions must be unlearned before new ones can be adopted

Dervin's Core Critique

The transmission model assumes that meaning inheres in messages — that the same message means the same thing to all receivers. SMM demonstrates this is empirically false: the same text, institution, or event generates radically different sense in different people in different situations. Designing for information transfer is, therefore, designing for failure.

Methodology in Practice
Research Design · Interviewing · Question Construction · Analysis

Research Paradigm

SMM is primarily a qualitative, interpretivist methodology — though Dervin explicitly designed it to be adaptable to quantitative survey instruments. The goal is always to get inside the sense-making moment of the participant, not to categorize them from the outside.

Core Research Commitment

SMM research asks participants about verbs, not nouns. Not "what topic did you need information about?" but "what question were you trying to answer?" Not "what information did you use?" but "what helped you move forward?" This verb-centered stance is what distinguishes SMM from topic-based information-seeking research.

The Micro-Moment Time-Line Interview (MMTLI)

Dervin's signature interviewing technique. It anchors the conversation in a specific, concrete moment — preventing the retrospective abstraction and generalization that plague most interviews.

1

Situating move: "Tell me about a specific time when you had to [navigate X / deal with Y / learn about Z]. Not in general — a specific moment."

2

Step-through move: "Walk me through what happened, step by step. Start at the beginning." The interviewer constructs a mental timeline of the narrative.

3

Gap probe: At each step: "At this point, what questions did you have? What was confusing? What were you trying to figure out?" This surfaces the gaps.

4

Bridge probe: "What helped you with that? What did you find or use? What did you draw on?" This identifies the bridges.

5

Outcome probe: "How did that help — or not help? What were you able to do as a result? What happened next?" This captures outcomes and uses.

6

Hurt probe: "Was there anything that got in the way? Anything that made it harder?" Eliciting negative outcomes and bridge failures is as important as eliciting successes.

Neutral Questioning Technique

Alongside the MMTLI, Dervin developed a questioning approach she called "neutral questioning." The neutrality is methodological, not political — it means questions are designed not to presuppose what kind of gap the person has, what bridges they should have used, or what outcomes they should value.

Non-Neutral (Avoid)Neutral (Use)Why It Matters
"What information did you need?""What questions did you have at that point?"Avoids presupposing information as the only bridge resource
"Did the library help you?""What helped you move forward?"Avoids privileging formal information systems
"Was the information useful?""How did that help — or not help?"Opens space for both helpful and unhelpful outcomes
"What topic were you researching?""What were you trying to do or figure out?"Frames the inquiry around the gap, not the topic
"Did you understand it?""What made sense to you? What remained confusing?"Separates understanding from use; avoids binary success/fail

Survey Adaptation

Dervin demonstrated that SMM could be operationalized in survey form using open-ended questions structured around situation → gap → bridge → outcome. Key design principles for SMM surveys:

  • Always ask about a specific recent occasion, not general patterns ("the last time you..." not "when you generally...")
  • Use funnel structure: situation first, then gap, then bridge, then outcome — never reverse this
  • Include both helped and hurt outcome questions — asymmetric success-only surveys produce distorted data
  • Allow emotional vocabulary in responses; do not code only cognitive/informational answers

How to Identify Gaps in User Narratives

In practice, gaps are rarely stated explicitly. Trained researchers listen for gap-markers — linguistic and narrative moves that signal discontinuity. The key question: where does forward movement stop?

Analytical Heuristic

Read (or listen to) the narrative and ask continuously: "At this point, what does this person not yet know, feel, or understand that they need in order to move forward?" The answer at each point is the gap. The resources they reach for next are the bridges. What changes as a result are the outcomes.

Specific gap-identification moves in analysis:

  • Mark every instance of "I didn't know," "I wasn't sure," "I was confused," "I had no idea" — these are explicit gap signals
  • Mark every question the participant reports asking (aloud or internally) — these are question-gaps
  • Mark every point where the narrative stalls, backtracks, or becomes fragmented — these are spin-out or overwhelming-gap markers
  • Mark every expression of frustration, fear, or distress — these signal barrier-gaps or dilemma-gaps
  • Mark every "but" or "however" — these often signal gap-bridge failure or unexpected outcomes
Applied Practice
UX · Strategy · Communications · Personal Decision-Making · Organizations

UX Research

SMM reframes UX research from "do users like this interface?" to "what gaps do users encounter, and what bridges does (or should) this interface provide?"

Traditional UX QuestionSMM-Framed UX Question
"Do users find the navigation intuitive?""At what moments do users encounter confusion? What do they reach for to resolve it?"
"What features do users want?""What gaps are users trying to bridge that current features don't address?"
"How satisfied are users?""When did this product help users move forward? When did it hurt or block them?"
"What is the user's journey?""Where in the journey do gaps emerge, and what bridges does the user construct (with or without the product)?"

In practice: Conduct MMTLI-style user interviews. Ask a participant to walk you through the last time they used the product for a specific purpose. At each step, probe for gaps (confusion, questions, frustration) and bridges (what helped, what they turned to). Design outputs: a gap map of the experience, annotated with bridge types. Design for gap-bridging, not feature delivery.

Strategy

Organizations frequently design strategies based on what leaders think stakeholders need to know — the transmission model. SMM offers a different frame: strategy as sense-making architecture.

Strategic Application

Before communicating strategy, map the sense-making landscape. Conduct SMM interviews with key stakeholders to understand: What situations are they in? What gaps do they have about the strategic direction? What bridges are they already constructing (often informal, partial, or inaccurate)? Strategy communication that addresses actual gaps is absorbed; strategy communication that transmits leadership-defined messages is ignored.

SMM-guided strategy process:

  • Gap audit: interview stakeholders using MMTLI about recent decisions or moments of uncertainty relevant to the strategy
  • Bridge inventory: identify what resources (formal and informal) people are already using to bridge their gaps
  • Outcome mapping: which existing bridges help? Which hurt? Which create new, unhelpful sense?
  • Design interventions: create communications, tools, and processes that bridge actual gaps rather than transmit desired messages

Communications

The transmission model dominates most professional communications practice. SMM inverts the design question: instead of "what do we need to tell them?" ask "what gaps do they have, and what bridges would help?"

Personal Decision-Making

SMM applied introspectively becomes a powerful tool for personal clarity. When facing a difficult decision:

S

Situate yourself: What is the actual situation you are in? What constraints, relationships, and histories are operative? Resist jumping to the gap before fully articulating the situation.

G

Name the gap precisely: Is this a decision-gap (choice between paths)? A barrier-gap (something is blocking me)? A confusion-gap (I don't understand what's happening)? Different gap types require different bridge strategies.

B

Inventory your bridges: What internal resources can you draw on (experience, values, intuition)? What external resources are available (information, people, systems)? Have you used sense-unmaking — challenged assumptions that may be shaping the gap incorrectly?

O

Anticipate outcomes: For each potential bridge, what are the plausible outcomes? Which help, which hurt, which create new gaps? This is not prediction — it is structured anticipation.

Organizational Problem-Solving

Organizations routinely attempt to solve problems by deploying information — reports, training, communications, systems — without diagnosing where actual sense-making gaps lie. SMM offers a diagnostic protocol:

Analytics & Digital Product Work

This is where I actually use SMM day-to-day. Quantitative analytics tells you what happened — where users dropped off, which variant won, which segment converts. SMM is the framework I reach for when I need to understand why. They are not competing methods; they are sequential ones.

The core problem SMM solves in analytics

Most analytics practices are optimised for measuring outcomes. A funnel chart shows drop-off at step 3. A heatmap shows where users stop scrolling. But none of this tells you what gap the user was experiencing at that moment — and therefore none of it tells you what bridge to build. SMM is the diagnostic layer that sits underneath quantitative data and makes it actionable.

Analytics SignalWhat Analytics Tells YouWhat SMM Asks Next
70% drop-off at checkout step 3Users are leaving at a specific point"Walk me through the last time you were trying to complete this purchase and something stopped you. What was happening at that moment?"
A/B test: Variant B wins by 12%B performs better on the target metric"What gap were users experiencing that Variant B bridged more effectively? What bridge did B provide that A didn't?"
Feature adoption at 8% after launchUsers are not using the new feature"Is the gap that this feature was designed to bridge actually the gap users have? Or did we design a bridge for an assumed gap rather than a real one?"
Support ticket volume spikes after redesignSomething broke in the user experience"What sense did users have of the old design that the redesign unmade? What new gaps did we create by dismantling their existing bridges?"
Session length drops in cohort analysisUsers are spending less time engaged"What changed in users' situations that shifted which gaps they're bringing to this product?"
Applied: Finding the Hypothesis When You Only Know the Question

This is a talk concept I've been developing — the moment in analytics work when you have a statistically significant finding but no idea what it means for the product. Quantitative data surfaces the fact of a gap. SMM provides the methodology for diagnosing its nature. You cannot design a bridge without knowing the gap type. A decision gap, a confusion gap, and a barrier gap at the same funnel step require entirely different interventions.

UX Telemetry + SMM: A Combined Protocol

UX telemetry (event tracking, session replay, heatmaps) gives you behavioural data at scale. SMM gives you the interpretive framework for that data. The combination is more powerful than either alone.

1

Instrument the product for gap signals. Beyond standard conversion events, instrument for frustration signals — rage clicks, repeated navigation attempts, search queries within the product, back-button behaviour. These are behavioural gap-markers: the telemetry equivalent of "I was lost," "I couldn't find," "something stopped me."

2

Cluster users by gap-signal patterns. Users who rage-click and back-navigate are experiencing something different from users who quietly exit. The gap taxonomy gives you a theoretically grounded way to segment: barrier gaps vs. confusion gaps vs. decision gaps produce distinct behavioural signatures in telemetry data.

3

Conduct targeted MMTLI interviews with each cluster. Sample 5-8 users from each behavioural cluster and run a micro-moment interview specifically about the session that produced the signal. You already know where the gap appeared in the journey — the telemetry told you. The MMTLI tells you its nature and what kind of bridge users wanted.

4

Design bridges for gap types, not for conversion metrics. A checkout abandonment at step 3 that stems from a trust gap requires a different bridge than one that stems from a logistics barrier gap. Design thinking generates the solution; SMM diagnosis specifies what problem the solution must solve.

Practice Projects — Try These Yourself

The best way to develop SMM fluency is to apply it to familiar contexts where you already have intuitions. These projects are designed to take 30-90 minutes each and produce something you can use or show.

P1
Analytics Practice
Gap-map a funnel you know

Take any multi-step funnel you have access to — a checkout flow, an onboarding sequence, a sign-up form. Identify the three steps with the highest drop-off. For each step, generate three hypotheses about the gap type using the taxonomy (decision, barrier, confusion, role, spin-out, washout). Then write one MMTLI-style question you would ask a user to test each hypothesis. Output: a gap hypothesis map with testable questions attached to each funnel step.

Time

45-60 minutes

What you'll produce

Gap hypothesis map + interview question set

SMM concept exercised

Gap taxonomy + neutral questioning

Output use

Research brief or portfolio artefact

P2
Survey Redesign
Rewrite a survey using SMM principles

Take any survey you have access to — NPS, post-purchase, user satisfaction, employee engagement. Read each question and identify: Is it asking about a situation, gap, bridge, or outcome? Is it presupposing the bridge type (e.g., "Was the information helpful?" presupposes information was the bridge)? Rewrite five questions using SMM-neutral framing. Use the Question Rewriter tab on this site to assist. Compare the original and rewritten versions — which would produce more actionable data?

Time

30-45 minutes

What you'll produce

Original vs. SMM-rewritten question set with rationale

SMM concept exercised

Neutral questioning + S-G-B-O structure

Output use

Survey methodology improvement; direct use in research

P3
Self-Interview
Apply the MMTLI to your own recent decision

Think of a significant decision you made in the last 3 months — a career choice, a purchase, a relationship decision, a project direction. Apply the MMTLI protocol to yourself: situate it, walk through it step by step, identify every gap you encountered (by type), name every bridge you used, and trace the outcomes. The goal is not therapy — it is to practise hearing the S-G-B-O structure in a narrative you know intimately. Most people are surprised by how many gaps they crossed unconsciously and how diverse their bridge types were.

Time

30 minutes writing, 15 minutes coding

What you'll produce

Annotated personal narrative with S-G-B-O coding

SMM concept exercised

Full MMTLI + gap identification + bridge typing

Output use

Interview skill development; methodological fluency

P4
A/B Test Post-Mortem
Diagnose a test result using gap-bridge framing

Take any A/B test result — one you ran, one from a public case study, one from a textbook. The standard analysis ends at "variant B won by X%." Apply SMM: What gap did each variant offer a bridge for? Was the winning variant providing a more effective bridge for the same gap, or was it bridging a different gap entirely? If variant B won, what sense did variant A fail to make for users? This reframe often reveals why a winning test still produces unexpectedly modest business results — because we measured bridge adoption, not gap resolution.

Time

45 minutes

What you'll produce

Gap-bridge analysis of test variants; reframed hypothesis

SMM concept exercised

Bridge theory + sense-unmaking + outcome typing

Output use

Stronger experimental hypotheses in future tests

Core Definitions

SMM
Sense-Making Methodology — Dervin's theoretical and methodological framework for studying how people construct meaning across discontinuities in time-space
Situation
The specific time-space context that shapes and constrains sense-making; not mere background but constitutive ground
Gap
The discontinuity interrupting forward movement; may be a question, confusion, barrier, dilemma, or absence; the engine of sense-making
Bridge
The sense constructed to cross a gap; may be informational, emotional, social, procedural, or cognitive; always constructed, never merely received
Outcome / Use
What results from bridging; includes helps, hurts, new gaps, and specific uses such as deciding, acting, connecting, evaluating
MMTLI
Micro-Moment Time-Line Interview — Dervin's signature interviewing technique anchored in a specific moment, walking through situation → gap → bridge → outcome
Neutral questioning
A questioning strategy that avoids presupposing gap type, bridge type, or outcome type; allows respondents to name their own experience
Sense-unmaking
The deliberate or necessary dismantling of existing sense; a complement to sense-making; required for learning, transformation, and critical thought
Discontinuity
The fundamental condition of reality in SMM's ontology; the world has gaps, and human beings spend their lives bridging them
Transmission model
The dominant alternative SMM critiques; treats information as an object that transfers from sender to receiver with meaning intact

Model Summary (One Paragraph)

SMM in Brief

A person moves through time-space in a specific situation. They encounter a gap — a discontinuity in their understanding or ability to move forward. To cross the gap they construct a bridge, drawing on internal resources (memory, emotion, knowledge) and external resources (information, people, systems). The bridge produces an outcome: it helps (movement continues), hurts (movement is blocked), or creates a new gap (requiring more bridging). Reality is fundamentally discontinuous; sense-making is the universal human practice of constructing bridges across that discontinuity. Sense-unmaking is the complementary practice of dismantling existing bridges when they no longer serve.

Essential Philosophical Assumptions (One Line Each)

  • Reality is discontinuous — gaps are not exceptions, they are the norm
  • Information is constructed in the act of bridging, not transmitted intact
  • Situation constitutes sense-making — context is not background, it is ground
  • People are active sense-makers, not passive receivers
  • Procedure is universal; sense made is unique to each situation
  • Subjectivity (emotion, intuition) is datum, not noise
  • Both internal and external orders are real and relevant

Key Principles for Practice

  • Always ask about specific moments, not general habits or topics
  • Probe for gap type before probing for bridge or outcome
  • Treat bridges neutrally — information is one bridge type among many
  • Always ask about hurts as well as helps — asymmetric outcome probing distorts findings
  • Sense-unmaking may be required before productive sense-making can begin
  • The same "information" will bridge different gaps differently depending on situation
  • Design for gap-bridging, not message-sending

Common Mistakes

Equating bridge with information.

Dervin explicitly resists this. Information is one kind of bridge resource — but bridges can be emotional resolutions, social connections, procedural steps, recalled memories, or creative leaps. Privileging information replicates the transmission model within SMM's frame.

Abstracting away from the situation.

SMM's power lies in its radical situatedness. Asking "what do users generally need?" rather than "what did this person need in this moment?" evacuates the methodology of its explanatory force.

Ignoring sense-unmaking.

Many SMM applications treat the model as purely constructive. But people often need to dismantle existing sense before they can make new sense. Omitting sense-unmaking produces an overly optimistic model that misses resistance, confusion, and transformative learning.

Treating gaps as deficits.

A gap is not a user's failure or ignorance to be corrected. It is the normal, expected discontinuity that characterizes human experience. Framing gaps as deficits reintroduces a transmission-model normative stance.

Using topic-based analysis.

Coding by topic ("users had questions about pricing") rather than gap type ("users faced a decision-gap requiring comparative information") loses SMM's analytical value and collapses back into traditional information-needs categorization.

Only probing positive outcomes.

Asking only "what helped?" misses the crucial data about what hurt, what failed, and what created worse gaps. Dervin explicitly built both valences into her methodology.

    SMM Question Rewriter
    Paste any survey, UX, or interview question — get it rewritten using Sense-Making principles

    How This Works

    Most research questions are written from the sender's perspective — they ask about topics, satisfaction ratings, or binary outcomes. SMM-informed questions ask about the person's situation, what stopped or confused them, and what helped them move forward. This tool rewrites any question using those principles.

    When to use this

    Survey design before fieldwork. UX interview guide review. A/B test hypothesis sharpening. Anywhere you're about to ask a human a question and want to make sure you're asking about their gap rather than your assumed topic.

    What Makes an SMM Question Different

    Original QuestionSMM RewriteWhat Changed
    "Did you find the information you needed?""Tell me about a moment in that process when you weren't sure what to do next. What were you trying to figure out?"Removes "information" assumption; opens gap to any bridge type; asks about a specific moment not a general assessment
    "How satisfied were you with the checkout?""Was there any point during checkout where something stopped you or made you uncertain? What happened at that point?"Moves from satisfaction rating to gap identification; surfaces actual friction rather than overall sentiment
    "What features do you want?""Think about the last time you were trying to accomplish something in this product and got stuck. What were you trying to do, and what would have helped you get past that moment?"Replaces abstract feature preference with specific gap-bridging need; grounds the answer in an actual situation

    Why SMM in Analytics Work

    Most analytics practices are measurement frameworks — they tell you what happened. Conversion rate, session length, feature adoption, cohort retention. These are all outcomes in SMM's framework. They tell you that a gap existed and whether a bridge was crossed. They do not tell you what the gap was, what kind of bridge users were looking for, or why the bridge worked for some users and not others.

    The moment I reach for SMM

    Every time I have a quantitative finding I cannot explain. A segment that converts at half the rate of every other segment. A feature that 91% of users never touch despite being prominently placed. An A/B test that wins statistically but produces no downstream business impact. These are all situations where telemetry has identified a gap but cannot diagnose its nature. SMM is the diagnostic framework for moving from "something is happening here" to "here is what the user is experiencing and what kind of bridge would actually help."

    The Talk: Finding the Hypothesis When You Only Know the Question

    This is a talk I am developing for the Amsterdam data and analytics community — specifically for the Netherlands dbt Meetup and DDMA Digital Analytics Summit audiences. The core argument: data analytics and qualitative user research are not competing methods. They are sequential ones. Analytics surfaces the fact of a gap. SMM provides the methodology for diagnosing its nature.

    Where This Shows Up in My Work

    1

    Measurement planning. Before any analytics implementation, I use the S-G-B-O framework to define what we are actually trying to measure. Not "we want to track page views" — but "users are in situation X, they encounter gap Y, what bridge does the product offer, and what outcome do we want to measure?" This produces a measurement plan that is theoretically grounded, not just technically complete.

    2

    Interview design for UX research. When I design research interviews alongside quantitative work, I use MMTLI structure. The quantitative data tells me where to look; the MMTLI tells me how to ask about what I find there. The neutral questioning technique is particularly important in analytics contexts — it is easy to contaminate an interview by presupposing that "information" was the bridge users were looking for.

    3

    Explaining why winning A/B tests sometimes don't move the business needle. Variant B won because it better bridged the gap users had at that step. But if the real business problem is that users are arriving with the wrong gap entirely — a problem that no variant in the test addressed — then all you have done is optimise the bridge for the wrong discontinuity. SMM provides the language for this diagnosis.

    4

    Sense-unmaking in data culture work. When I build analytics infrastructure at organisations that have been operating without it, the hardest part is never technical. It is dismantling the existing sense structures that leaders have built about how their business works — usually based on incomplete or distorted data. Dervin's sense-unmaking concept is exactly what describes this: before new sense can be made, old sense must be deliberately dismantled.
